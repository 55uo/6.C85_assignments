Assignment 4 Persuasive or Deceptive Visualization?

Yi Suo — ssuo@mit.edu

Proposition: States with restrictive abortion laws correlate with lower in-state abortion rates

FOR the Proposition

Design Decisions and Rationale:

  • Creating a Restrictiveness Index and Grouping into Percentiles (Score: +2): I identified five key factors that impact access to abortion clinics (e.g., clinic closures, provider density, travel rates), normalized these measures, and then computed a weighted average to form an access restrictiveness index. Here, accessibility refers to how easily residents can obtain abortion services, while restrictiveness captures the extent of policy barriers limiting access. I divided the index into eight percentile groups, from “Most Accessible” to “Most Restrictive”, to capture subtle differences across states. This decision is data-driven and transparent, which makes my visualization persuasive because it reflects a nuanced understanding of policy impacts. I considered using fewer groups, but settling on eight allowed for a more detailed narrative.
  • Filtering Out Outliers That Contradicted the Trend (Score: -1): I filtered out outlier states that fell outside the interquartile range (IQR) in the bar chart, as these states did not follow the overall trend of lower in‐state abortion rates with increased restrictiveness. This filtering helped clarify the downward trend and strengthened the persuasive message, although it does omit some valid data points. I weighed the alternative of including all data, but including these outliers would have muddled the pattern I was trying to emphasize.
  • Title Implication of a “Divide” (Score: +0.5): I opted for a title that subtly hints at a “geographic divide,” linking clusters of policies (for example, a more restrictive South) to the observed outcomes without directly claiming causation. This decision helps make my visualization persuasive by drawing attention to regional differences while staying technically accurate. I considered a neutral title like “Policy Distribution by State,” but that alternative would have lacked the persuasive impact I was aiming for.
  • Color Choices (Score: +0.5): I selected a red-to-blue gradient for the map visualization to differentiate levels of policy restrictiveness. Using red to indicate more restrictive policies and blue for more accessible ones leverages familiar political color associations, making the map immediately understandable and persuasive. This approach worked well in drawing attention and providing clarity, though I acknowledge that these colors may also evoke certain emotional responses. I considered a neutral, single-hue palette, but the red-to-blue scheme was ultimately more effective at communicating the intended message.
  • Averaging Rates Without Weighting by Population (Score: -1.5): I calculated average in‐state abortion rates without adjusting for state population sizes. This decision means that small states with extreme values (like Wyoming) can disproportionately affect the averages, reinforcing the narrative that restrictiveness correlates with lower in‐state abortion rates. Although using weighted averages would have offered a more balanced view, I chose the unweighted method because it better supports the persuasive argument I was making, despite its limitations.

AGAINST the Proposition

Design Decisions and Rationale:

  • Using 100% Stacked Bars to Emphasize Proportional Dependency (Score: -1): I chose a 100% stacked bar chart to illustrate the proportion of in-state versus out-of-state abortions, thereby spotlighting how much each group relies on external access. By adding the absolute counts for each segment, I reinforce the point that states in more restrictive categories aren’t necessarily experiencing fewer total abortions—they’re just pushing them elsewhere. I considered using a dual-axis chart (counts plus proportions), but that would have been more complex and less direct in conveying the argument that restrictiveness shifts, rather than reduces, abortions.
  • Weighted Averages for Out-of-State % (Score: +2): I applied a weighting scheme based on each group’s total abortion volume so that populous states (e.g., Texas) carry more influence than smaller states (e.g., Wyoming). This approach offers a more realistic depiction of the broader impact, making the argument more convincing: when large states have higher out-of-state percentages, it’s a clear sign of displacement. I considered using simple averages, but that would let small outlier states skew the results.
  • Low-Contrast Color Encoding for Out-of-State Segments (Score: -0.5): I opted for a subdued purple shade for out-of-state abortions, which slightly underplays their prominence. While this choice helps keep the visualization from feeling too confrontational, it also risks diminishing the apparent magnitude of out-of-state reliance. I briefly considered a bold, contrasting palette but felt it might overshadow the data, so I stuck with a softer approach that still highlights the displacement narrative.
  • Title Framing (“Rely More”) to Undermine the Proposition (Score: +1): I used the phrase “Rely More” in the title to shift the interpretation from “restrictive laws reduce abortions” to “restrictive laws push abortions out of state.” This directly challenges the idea that fewer in-state procedures indicate fewer overall abortions, instead suggesting that people are simply forced to seek services elsewhere. A neutral title like “Out-of-State Abortion Rates” would have been less persuasive for an against-the-proposition viewpoint.
  • Ordering Groups from Most to Least Restrictive (Score: +1.5): I arranged the bars so that groups become more restrictive from left to right, creating a clear progression showing how out-of-state reliance increases with policy stringency. This mirrors the layout of the For visualization, making it easy to compare how the same data can support an opposing argument. I considered sorting by total abortion volume, but decided that emphasizing restrictiveness directly was more impactful for highlighting the displacement effect.

    • Final Reflection

    Throughout this process, the initial step of identifying key data points, such as in-state versus out-of-state abortions and the factors that contribute to the restrictiveness index, seemed clear at first, even though the dataset didn’t explicitly provide these figures. I invested a significant amount of time working through calculations and mathematical formulations to derive the necessary metrics. In contrast, striking the right balance between persuasion and honesty proved much more challenging. I quickly assembled the pro chart, but when I turned to the against chart, every calculation I attempted still supported the original proposition. To overcome this, I shifted my perspective and subtly redirected the audience’s attention to a related narrative, one that emphasizes how restrictive policies displace abortions rather than reduce overall demand.

    Decisions like filtering out outliers, selecting specific color schemes, and framing titles with emotive language pushed the boundaries of what felt like a fair representation of the data. I was surprised by how a minor tweak, such as removing just a few outlier states, could radically alter the perceived trend. I also dedicated significant effort to refining the title and color scheme to ensure the focus shifted appropriately. This experience underscored how even small design choices can powerfully shape a narrative.

    I now define “ethical analysis and visualization” as the practice of being transparent about design decisions and fully acknowledging their impact on the audience’s interpretation. There is a fine line between acceptable persuasive choices, such as emphasizing a particular trend with a clear, data-backed rationale, and misleading ones, like distorting axes or omitting significant contradictory data without explanation. In my view, an acceptable approach conveys the data’s fundamental truth, while a misleading one obscures it. Ultimately, acknowledging and documenting the motivations behind each design decision is essential for maintaining ethical standards, even when the objective is to persuade.
