Reading & Critique

Insights about the data/visualization:

  • The spiral design effectively aligns the same months of different years along the same half-axis, which makes it easier to compare case trends across corresponding time periods. This design helps users recognize patterns and seasonal recurrences more intuitively.
  • The visualization reveals that COVID-19 cases surge most significantly in winter, particularly following the December holidays. This suggests that factors such as increased indoor gatherings and holiday travel contribute to higher transmission rates, potentially overwhelming individual immunity and accelerating the spread of the disease.
  • The widening spiral visually emphasizes the sharp increase in cases from 2020 to 2022. This reinforces the impact of new variants, changes in immunity levels, and shifting public health responses over time.
  • The use of a 7-day moving average helps highlight broader trends by reducing short-term volatility in case numbers. This makes it easier to distinguish long-term waves from daily noise, allowing viewers to focus on meaningful patterns rather than being distracted by fluctuations in case reporting.

Design Critique:

  • This spiral visualization is visually striking and effectively conveys the magnitude and recurrence of COVID-19 surges. However, it has several drawbacks that limit its clarity and impact. The spiral layout, while unique, lacks a sense of urgency that a public health crisis demands. The soft red shading and smooth curves give the data an artistic appearance rather than emphasizing the severity of the outbreaks. Additionally, while the design highlights seasonal trends, it sacrifices the ability to make direct comparisons of case counts across different time periods. A standard line chart, for instance, would offer a clearer and more intuitive view of peaks and troughs over time.
  • Another issue is the distortion caused by the radial layout. Some months appear stretched, making them seem longer or more significant than others, which can mislead viewers about the duration and intensity of each wave. This effect, combined with the lack of numerical markers beyond the single 150K reference point, makes it difficult to extract precise values. Adding labels for key peaks, minima, or even additional reference lines could improve readability and provide better context. While the spiral format effectively emphasizes seasonality, a more traditional time-series chart would likely offer more precise insights into the rate of increase and decline for each wave, making it a better tool for data-driven decision-making.

Visualization Sketches

Design Rationale for Sketch 1:

  • I went with a standard time-series line chart because it gives a much clearer view of peaks and troughs over time. This directly fixes the readability and comparability issues from the spiral design, making it easier to track when cases were rising or falling.
  • Instead of mapping data along a warped radial path, I laid it out in a way that maintains consistent spacing between months. This eliminates the issue of distorted time perception while preserving seasonality, making it much easier to compare year-over-year trends without visual bias.
  • I also added annotations for key events, like the Omicron outbreak, so it’s obvious when external factors contributed to major case spikes. This helps restore the sense of urgency that was lost in the spiral’s smooth curves—sudden surges now stand out, making it clear when outbreaks were accelerating or slowing down.

Design Rationale for Sketch 2:

  • I went for a bar chart because it simplifies year-over-year comparisons while preserving the seasonal patterns seen in the original spiral. By aggregating data into seasons, I removed the noise of month-to-month fluctuations and instead focused on the bigger picture: how outbreaks aligned with colder months and holiday seasons.
  • I intentionally chose warm and cool colors to match the feel of each season—cool blue for winter, fresh green for spring, warm orange for summer, and earthy brown for fall. This not only makes the chart visually appealing but also reinforces the seasonal groupings at a glance, helping viewers intuitively associate trends with different times of the year.
  • I added a partial outline around the 2022 bars to subtly separate the seasons without breaking the overall flow of the graph. This keeps things organized and makes it easier to read across different seasons without bars blending into each other.

Design Rationale for Sketch 3:

  • I wanted to keep the cyclical nature of COVID-19 waves but fix the issues from the original spiral design. Instead of a continuous curve, I structured the data into concentric rings, with each ring representing a different year. This makes it easier to compare the same months across different years without distortion, while still emphasizing how outbreaks followed seasonal patterns.
  • The yellow-to-deep-red gradient effectively highlights case severity, making major surges immediately visible. The structured circular layout helps track recurring outbreaks, and the even spacing of months ensures no single time period looks exaggerated. The reference legend also helps translate color into case counts, making the data more interpretable at a glance.
  • While the design successfully conveys seasonality, precise case values are still hard to extract. Adding numerical labels or key event annotations (like Omicron’s peak) could provide more context. I’d also explore thicker gridlines or subtle radial guides to further improve readability, and possibly test a logarithmic color scale to better differentiate between moderate and extreme case counts.

Final Visualization Design

Design Process & Design Decisions

This final design came after multiple iterations where I tried to balance clarity, seasonality, and meaningful context. I started off with the spiral visualization, which was great for emphasizing the cyclical nature of COVID-19 waves, but I quickly realized it distorted time and made direct comparisons between waves difficult. I then experimented with bar charts for seasonal aggregation and radial heatmaps to keep the cyclical aspect while improving readability, but those didn’t do a great job of showing the precise timing and scale of case surges. Eventually, I landed on a time-series line and area chart hybrid, which gives a clean, structured view while still preserving big-picture insights.

The stacked area chart for new cases helps show the sheer scale and duration of each wave, while the red line chart for new deaths makes it easy to compare infection waves with their fatality impact. I also wanted to bring back the seasonality aspect that got lost in standard time-series charts, so I added subtle background shading for each season, using colors that intuitively map to the time of year. This helps reinforce the idea that certain seasons—especially winter—saw repeated surges. Another big improvement was adding vertical reference lines to mark major events like the first U.S. lockdown, vaccine approvals, and major variant peaks. These provide much-needed context for why some waves were more severe than others. I also used different line styles to distinguish policy-related events from variant-driven peaks, making it clear what type of event each reference line represents.

Since raw daily case numbers can be pretty noisy, I used a 14-day moving average to smooth things out and highlight longer-term trends. I also paid close attention to the axis scales to make sure both cases and deaths could be shown without one overshadowing the other—hence the dual-axis setup, which makes it easier to see how deaths lag behind case surges. The color scheme took a few tries to get right. I originally considered using a simple diverging scale but ended up layering gradients for seasonality while using bold, distinct colors for reference lines to reinforce the key takeaways. The deep blue for case trends keeps them visually dominant, while the red death trend is clear without being overpowering. Looking at the final result, I think this design does a solid job of balancing detail with readability, making it both informative and easy to interpret.

Reflection

This final design successfully addresses many of the critiques I had about the original spiral visualization while also revealing new design challenges along the way. One of the biggest weaknesses of the spiral was that it distorted time, making it difficult to directly compare case counts across different waves. This time-series format eliminates that issue by presenting trends in a structured, linear way, allowing for much clearer comparisons between different periods. The spiral also lacked clear contextual markers, which made it harder to interpret why certain surges occurred. I resolved this by adding vertical reference lines for major pandemic milestones, such as lockdowns, vaccine rollouts, and variant peaks, providing a much-needed link between policy interventions and case trends. Additionally, the dual-axis setup improves readability, making it easier to compare case surges and deaths without one overshadowing the other.

However, working through this redesign process also reinforced that some critiques are harder to fully resolve. One challenge was maintaining the emphasis on seasonality without introducing unnecessary complexity. The spiral format naturally conveyed cyclicality, whereas a standard time-series chart does not. To address this, I added seasonal shading in the background, but this also made the chart look a bit busier than I originally intended. I experimented with alternative ways to encode seasonality, such as separate seasonal averages or statistical breakdowns, but these disrupted the overall flow of the time-series design. This was the best balance I could find between keeping the focus on trends while still showing the recurring seasonal patterns. Another limitation is that while this version improves precision, it still prioritizes trends over exact numerical comparisons, meaning it might not be ideal for someone looking for more statistical detail. Still, this process reinforced how every design decision—whether it’s color, layout, annotations, or axis formatting—shapes the way data is understood. In the end, while not every critique could be perfectly addressed, this final version significantly improves on the clarity, comparability, and contextual depth that were missing in the earlier designs.
